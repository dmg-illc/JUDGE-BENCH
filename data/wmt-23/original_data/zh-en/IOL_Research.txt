4 Vocabulary
4.1 Overview
The characters in this file are encoded in single-byte, double-word, or quad-byte encodings.
The single-byte part contains all 128 characters from 0x00 to 0x7F of GB/T 11383-1989.
4.3 Double-byte parts
The double-byte part contains all graphic characters from GB/T 2312-1980 and CJK unified Chinese characters from GB/T 13000, as well as some graphic characters.
Characters in the double-byte portion are as specified in Appendix A.
The graphics, code locations, and functions of the ideograph descriptors shall comply with the provisions of Appendix B.
Note: GB/T 13000 is the unified encoding of Chinese characters used in China, Japan, Enguo, Vietnam and other countries and regions.
Characters with unique abstract glyphs are assigned a separate code position; characters with the same abstract glyph from different sources are assigned a common code position.
The encoded Chinese characters are called CJK Unified Ideographs, where CJK stands for China, Japan, and South Korea.
4.4 Four-byte parts
The four-byte part contains 66 CJK Unified Chinese Characters (9FA6-9FEF, excluding 8 characters from 9FB4 to 9FBB) in GB/T 13000 in addition to the above-mentioned double-byte characters, CJK Unified Chinese Character Extension A, CJK Unified Chinese Character Extension BCJK Unified Chinese Character Extension C, CJK Unified Chinese Character Extension DCJK Unified Chinese Character Extension .CJK Unified Chinese Character Extension F, and characters from ethnic minority scripts already encoded in GB/T 13000.
Characters in the four-byte section are as specified in Appendix C.
5 General structure
Hexadecimal is used for numbers preceded by 0x in the text, and decimal is used for numbers not preceded by 0x.
All codes in the appendix are represented in hexadecimal, and all other numbers are represented in decimal.
The single-byte part uses the GB/T 11383-1989 encoding structure, so that the 0x00~0x7F code points are used.
The two-character tribute part uses two eight-bit binary bit strings to represent a character, with the first byte code point from 0x81-0xFE and the last byte code points from 0x40-0x7E and 0x80-0xFE, respectively.
The four-byte part uses 0x30-0x39, which was not used in GB/WP 11383-1989, as a suffix to expand the two-byte encoding to 0x81308130-0xFE39FE39.
The first character of a four-byte character is encoded in the range 0x81-0xFE; the second character is encoded in the range 0x30-0x39; the third character is encoded in the range 0x81-0xFE; and the fourth character is encoded in the range 0x30-0x39. The executive summary
Impact of Zika on the drug market
The impact could be similar to the 2008 economic crisis
The global pandemic has plunged the world into an unprecedented crisis.
In many countries, the disease has overwhelmed health services to the point of collapse, and restrictions imposed by Governments have caused serious disruptions in the global economy.
For the drug market, the impact of the outbreak is unclear and difficult to predict, but it is likely to be far-reaching.
Some producers may be forced to find new ways to make drugs, as restrictions on movement also limit access to precursors and essential chemicals.
Traffickers, unable to cross borders because of travel restrictions, may have to find new routes and methods.
Patterns of drug use and availability may change, and the ability of governments to respond may be hampered.
The transformation brought about by COVID-19 is unprecedented, but there are lessons to be learned from previous crises.
After the 2008 economic crisis, some drug users began looking for cheaper synthetic substances, and patterns of use shifted to injecting drugs.
At the same time, governments have cut drug-related budgets.
Interventions such as prevention of drug use and related risk behaviours and drug treatment services could be hit hard if governments respond to the current economic downturn in the same way.
In addition, interdiction operations and international cooperation may be given lower priority, making it easier for traffickers to operate.
The direct impact on drug trafficking is expected to be greatest in countries where large quantities of drugs are smuggled on commercial flights.
Synthetic drugs such as methamphetamine are carried by couriers, sometimes in large quantities, in carry-on backpacks or concealed in personal luggage, and the use of commercial air travel for the supply of drugs may be completely disrupted as a result of restrictions on air passenger transport almost everywhere.
While the international postal supply chain has also been disrupted, the use of the dark web and the mailing of goods for trafficking is likely to increase.
In the long run, the recession and related closures could disrupt the drug market.
As a result of rising unemployment and lack of opportunities, poor and vulnerable people will be more likely to use drugs in harmful ways, develop drug use disorders and turn to illicit drug-related activities, whether production or transportation.
Drug-trafficking organizations may take advantage of this situation to provide services to vulnerable populations and recruit new members to strengthen their ranks.
As governments become less able to respond, these shifts could quickly take root and become the new reality for many communities.
Expansion and complexity
Market growth
Population growth patterns partly explain the expansion of the market
Drug abuse has been increasing around the world, both in terms of overall numbers and in terms of the proportion of drug users in the world population.
In 2009, there were an estimated 210 million drug users, or 4.8 per cent of the global population aged 15-64, while in 2018 there were an estimated 269 million drug users, or 5.3 per cent of this population.
Over the past two decades, drug use in developing countries has grown much faster than in developed countries.
This partly reflects the difference in overall population growth during the period, which was 7 per cent in developed countries and 28 per cent in developing countries, but also reflects the faster growth of the younger population in developing countries.
Adolescents and young adults make up the largest proportion of drug users.
Between 2000 and 2018, the population of this age group increased by 16 per cent in developing countries, while it decreased by 10 per cent in developed countries.
1 Urbanization as a driver of the current and future drug market
In both developed and developing countries, drug use is higher in urban areas than in rural areas.
The overall increase in drug use is partly due to the large-scale movement of people from rural to urban areas. More than half of the world ' s population now lives in urban areas, compared with 34 per cent in 1960.
Urbanization is likely to be a key factor in the future dynamics of drug markets, particularly in developing countries where urbanization trends are more pronounced.
Increased wealth is associated with increased drug use, but it is the poorest who bear the greatest burden of disease. Worldwide, drug use is more prevalent in developed countries than in developing countries.
Drugs such as cocaine are more strongly associated with the wealthier parts of the world.
Similarly, within countries, the prevalence of drug use is higher among the wealthier segments of society.
However, it is the lower socio-economic groups that have a higher rate of conversion to drug abuse.
Data from a small number of countries suggest a link between harmful drug use patterns and conditions and low income.
These patterns seem to be less common in wealthier social strata.
The economic constraints caused by the global COVID-19 crisis are likely to exacerbate the risks for the most vulnerable groups, including drug users.
For example, changes in the labour market, such as rising unemployment, have in the past been associated with increased drug use, and the pandemic has forced tens of millions of people out of work globally.
A pandemic may lead to a further expansion of the drug market, which may result in more farmers increasing or engaging in illicit crop cultivation, either because of the reduced control capacity of national authorities or because more people may have to resort to illegal activities in the face of the economic crisis.
Trafficking by sea may have increased as a result of the reduction in air and land traffic as a result of COVID-19-related restrictions, as the risk of interception at sea is low and the volume of smuggling is greater than by air or land.
There have been recent reports of cocaine shipments directly from South America to Europe by sea.
Increased complexity
The emergence of non-internationally controlled substances is stable, but potentially harmful new opioids are proliferating and the drug market is becoming increasingly complex.
In addition to plant-based substances such as cannabis, cocaine and heroin, hundreds of synthetic drugs have been added, many of which are not under international control, and the non-medical use of drugs is also increasing rapidly.
There are about 500 new active substances on the domestic market of the Member States every year.
Currently most of these are stimulants, followed by synthetic cannabinoid receptor agonists and a small number of opioids.
While the overall number of new psychoactive substances has stabilized, the proportion has changed.
Opioid new psychoactive substances accounted for only 2 per cent of the total number of new psychoactive substance types identified in 2014, but by 2018 that figure had risen to 9 per cent.
Many of the new opioid psychoactive substances are analogues of fentanyl, whose efficacy and harm have been shown to cause overdose deaths in North America and, to a lesser extent, in other regions.
In North America, fentanyl is used either as an adulterant for heroin and other drugs, including cocaine and methamphetamine, or to manufacture counterfeit pharmaceutical opioids.
There is some evidence that stimulant injections of new psychoactive substances are also taking place in Europe: a study of the residues of discarded syringes in six European cities found that many were contaminated with stimulant-producing new psychoactive substances.
The use of new psychoactive substances may gradually become entrenched in vulnerable populations
A single type of new psychoactive substance almost never forms a large market.
But evidence from Europe suggests that synthetic cannabinoids are a serious problem among marginalized groups such as the homeless and prison inmates.
Twenty-two countries reported on the use of new psychoactive substances in prisons, most of which identified synthetic cannabinoids as a major challenge.
Control of precursor chemicals forces drug makers to innovate Many of the chemicals most commonly used as precursors to synthesize drugs such as amphetamine, methamphetamine and "ecstasy" have been placed under international control.
Drug traffickers and producers have been looking for alternatives not only to less regulated substances, but also to chemicals specifically designed to circumvent controls, so-called "tailored precursors".
Restrictive measures aimed at stopping the spread of Zika are likely to further squeeze producers who rely on precursor chemicals.
Evidence from Mexico suggests that this is already the case: in March 2020, a reported shortage of methamphetamine precursors imported from East Asia prompted methamphetamine prices to rise in Mexico and the United States.
The market is changing rapidly
Synthetic drugs replace opiates in Central Asia and the Russian Federation
The opiate market in Central Asia and the Russian Federation appears to have changed between 2008 and 2018.
The volume of opiates intercepted by the authorities of the Russian Federation has decreased by about 80 per cent, while the number of people receiving treatment for opioid use has decreased significantly.
But the market for stimulants seems to be booming.
There is evidence that methamphetamine and various cathinones, including mephedrone and a-pyrrolidone, are now widely available on the Russian drug market.
Russian authorities have reported a sharp rise in the number of clandestine laboratories manufacturing various illicit drugs recently seized, more than doubling in two years to 152 laboratories in 2018.
Methamphetamine market growth in Afghanistan and Iraq
As early as 2012, surveys of personnel in hospitals, prisons and other institutions had revealed the importance of methamphetamine in Iraq.
Crystalline methamphetamine has become a major drug of concern in addition to fentanyl and tramadol.
A 2015 study further corroborated these findings, with users saying cannabis was harder to obtain than fentanyl or methamphetamine.
Recently, the Iraqi authorities discovered a number of methamphetamine laboratories, and the Board has expressed concern about the large-scale importation of pseudoephedrine preparations, which are used as precursors by one methamphetamine laboratory.
In Afghanistan, methamphetamine manufacturing appears to have started in 2014.
Since then, methamphetamine seizures have risen steadily.
But seizures in the first half of 2019 amounted to 1^657 kilograms - a sharp increase compared to the previous year.
Large seizures of methamphetamine believed to have originated in Afghanistan in other countries also indicate that production in that country is rising rapidly.
Policy changes and trends
Most jurisdictions that have legalized the non-medical use of cannabis have seen an increase in cannabis use
Eleven jurisdictions in Canada, Uruguay and the United States allow the manufacture of cannabis products for non-medical use.
In most of these jurisdictions, cannabis use has increased since legalization, although the same trend has been observed in other jurisdictions that have not legalized the non-medical use of cannabis.
In Colorado and Washington, two of the first states to pass legislation in the United States, non-medical use of cannabis by adults has increased, particularly in the past month and on a daily or near-daily basis, exceeding the national average since 2012.
All indicators show that cannabis use in Uruguay has increased since 2011, with use almost doubling in the last month.
This suggests that the main reason for the increase is the regular and frequent use of cannabis.
Short-term data from Canada also suggest that the expansion of the legal market from 2018 to 2019 has been accompanied by an increase in usage.
However, 40 per cent of cannabis users in 2019 still relied on illicit sources to obtain some of their products.
Is the legalization of cannabis behind the collapse in global cannabis herb seizures?
Global seizures of cannabis herb fell to their lowest level in 20 years in 2018, driven by a decline in seizures in North America, which have fallen by 84 per cent over the past decade.
In contrast, seizures in the rest of the world nearly doubled over the same period. The pattern of seizures suggests that policies aimed at liberalizing the cannabis market played a key role in the decline.
